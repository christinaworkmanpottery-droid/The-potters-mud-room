🎨 FEATURED TECHNIQUE: Wax Resist Layering for Spring Glazes

Spring calls for fresh, organic patterns — and wax resist
layering is the perfect technique to get botanical, flowing
designs without any fancy tools.

How It Works:
1. Bisque fire your piece as usual.
2. Apply your base glaze — dip or pour a full coat of your
   lighter color (think celadon, cream, or a soft blue).
3. Paint wax resist where you want to preserve the base color.
   Think leaves, vines, abstract lines, or simple circles.
   Use a cheap brush you don't love — wax is hard on brushes.
4. Apply your second glaze over the entire piece. The wax
   repels it, revealing your base color underneath in your
   design.
5. Fire and enjoy! The contrast between the two glazes creates
   beautiful depth.

Pro Tips:
• Let your wax dry COMPLETELY (15-20 min) before applying the
  second glaze. Rushing = smudges.
• Keep your wax resist warm but never hot — set the jar in
  warm water if it thickens up.
• For extra dimension, try a third glaze wash over everything
  before firing. The wax burns off in the kiln, so it only
  affects the raw glaze layer.
• Practice your brush strokes on newspaper first. Wax resist
  is NOT forgiving — once it's on, it's on.

Log your wax resist experiments in The Mud Room's glaze combo

🔧 STUDIO HACK OF THE MONTH: The Zip-Lock Drying System

Tired of pieces cracking because they dried unevenly? Try this
dead-simple hack:

1. After forming, place your piece on a small bat or tile.
2. Loosely drape a thin plastic bag (like a grocery bag) over
   it — don't seal it tight.
3. After 24 hours, poke 2-3 small holes in the bag with a
   pencil.
4. Every day, add 2-3 more holes until the bag looks like
   Swiss cheese.
5. After 4-5 days, remove the bag entirely.

This creates a controlled slow-dry environment that lets
moisture escape gradually and evenly. It's especially clutch
for:
• Large platters and bowls with thick bottoms
• Pieces with handles or attachments
• Anything with uneven wall thickness
• Dry studio environments or hot weather

Cost: $0. Effort: Minimal. Cracking reduction: Massive.
